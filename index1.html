    Example: Projectile Motion

    Step 1: Resolve initial velocity into components.

    v_x = v * cos(θ)

    v_y = v * sin(θ)

    Step 2: Calculate time of flight.

    t = (2 * v_y) / g

    Step 3: Calculate range.

    R = v_x * t

    Step 4: Maximum height.

    H = (v_y²) / (2g)

    Additional Details:

    The projectile motion problem involves analyzing the motion of an object launched into the air, subject only to gravity. The initial velocity vector is broken into horizontal (v_x) and vertical (v_y) components using trigonometric functions, where θ is the launch angle relative to the horizontal.

    Assumptions:

    • Air resistance is negligible.
    • Gravity (g) is constant at approximately 9.81 m/s².
    • The launch and landing points are at the same height (y = 0).

    Step-by-Step Derivation:

    1. Horizontal Motion: The horizontal component of velocity (v_x) remains constant since there are no horizontal forces (ignoring air resistance). Thus, the horizontal distance (range) is calculated as R = v_x * t, where t is the total time of flight.

    2. Vertical Motion: The vertical component of velocity (v_y) is affected by gravity. The time to reach the peak (where vertical velocity is zero) is t_peak = v_y / g. Since the motion is symmetric, the total time of flight is twice this: t = 2 * v_y / g.

    3. Maximum Height: At the peak, the vertical kinetic energy is converted to potential energy. Using the kinematic equation v² = u² + 2as with final velocity v = 0 at the peak, initial velocity u = v_y, and acceleration a = -g, we get H = (v_y²) / (2g).

    4. Practical Example: Suppose a projectile is launched with an initial velocity v = 20 m/s at an angle θ = 30°. First, calculate v_x = 20 * cos(30°) ≈ 17.32 m/s and v_y = 20 * sin(30°) = 10 m/s. Then, time of flight t = (2 * 10) / 9.81 ≈ 2.04 s. Range R = 17.32 * 2.04 ≈ 35.33 m. Maximum height H = (10²) / (2 * 9.81) ≈ 5.10 m.

    Additional Notes:

    This solution assumes a flat, level surface and no external forces other than gravity. For more complex scenarios, such as those involving air resistance or varying terrain, additional calculations or numerical methods may be required.

    Further Considerations:

    - Energy Conservation: The total mechanical energy (kinetic + potential) is conserved in the absence of air resistance. At launch, the kinetic energy is (1/2)mv², and at the peak, the energy is converted to potential energy mgh.

    - Trajectory Shape: The path of a projectile is a parabola under these ideal conditions, derived from the combination of constant horizontal velocity and uniformly accelerated vertical motion.

    - Applications: Projectile motion is relevant in various fields, such as ballistics, sports (e.g., basketball, soccer), and engineering (e.g., designing launch systems).
